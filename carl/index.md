Hi, I'm Carl.

**TL;DR:** I've spent the last twenty years trying to measure things that are hard to measure: soil erosion in a Tanzanian village, sustainability on millions of smallholder coffee farms, and lately the impact of open source software.

## The short version

I started college on a computer science track and left with an environmental science degree and a thesis that used satellite data to predict soil erosion in a Tanzanian village. That turned into a few years of living and working in Tanzania, then a decade in coffee — first at [TechnoServe](https://www.technoserve.org/), helping cooperatives across East Africa build businesses and get financing, then as a cofounder of [Enveritas](https://enveritas.org), which verifies sustainability conditions on coffee farms in more than 20 countries. In 2022 I switched domains entirely and joined [Protocol Labs](https://protocol.ai) to work on [Hypercerts](https://hypercerts.org), a data standard for rewarding impact. Today I run [Kariba Labs](https://www.karibalabs.co/), where we build [Open Source Observer](https://www.opensource.observer), an open data platform that helps foundations and ecosystem funds measure the impact of open source software.

## The long version

My first real job was knocking on doors for [Clean Water Action](https://www.cleanwateraction.org/) in high school — four hours a night, forty doors, asking people across Rhode Island to care about clean drinking water. I was chased off exactly one doorstep by a man with a rifle. I even [wrote about it](../essays/cleaner_world) in the local paper. I've been knocking on doors for causes I care about, in one form or another, ever since.

I went to college planning to study computer science and, two years in, switched to environmental science (with a minor in a field called Science & Society — a blend of philosophy of science and economics). A semester abroad landed me in Kambi ya Simba, a village in northern Tanzania's Rift Valley highlands, and my [thesis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/253841943_Estimating_Soil_Loss_Using_ASTER_and_Diffuse_Reflectance_Spectrometry_a_Case_Study_of_the_Village_of_Kambi_ya_Simba_in_Northern_Tanzania's_Rift_Valley_Highlands) used satellite imagery and soil spectrometry to estimate erosion there. I mailed my final report to the village, and nothing happened. It was my first lesson in the difference between measuring a problem and doing something useful about it.

So after graduation I raised [a few small grants](../essays/diy_peacecorps) to go back — what I fondly call my DIY Peace Corps. I learned Swahili, helped start a tree nursery and a water pump, and eventually launched a microloan program that grew to about 500 customers. Then, at 24, in the span of 48 hours, I was fired, had my residence permit cancelled, and had to walk away from the community I'd spent three years with. [The full story](../essays/the-most-important-lesson-of-my20s) involves an unsent "mean letter" and a lot of expensive phone calls with my mom, who talked me out of waging a war I would have regretted. I moved back in with my parents and started plotting.

A few months later, my former housemate in Tanzania called about a job on a coffee project. I never formally interviewed — I happened to know Swahili, had a decent head for numbers, and liked running farmer trainings. That's how I ended up at [TechnoServe](https://www.technoserve.org/), where my first assignment was helping a group of farmers broker an export financing deal and sell their coffee to Starbucks. The work went well enough that the Gates Foundation eventually gave TechnoServe its largest grant ever to scale it, and the [Coffee Initiative](https://www.technoserve.org/our-work/projects/coffee-initiative/) went on to reach a quarter of a million farming households across East Africa. I spent those years in Kenya, Rwanda, and above all Ethiopia, helping over 100 cooperatives build wet mills and secure [bank financing](https://pressroom.ifc.org/all/pages/PressDetail.aspx?ID=22203) — work that was equal parts spreadsheets, [swollen rivers](../essays/busa), and long drives down [potholed roads I still miss](../essays/agaro).

I loved the work, but I was never comfortable with the expat lifestyle that came with it ([more on that here](../essays/usaid)). I worried that if I stayed on the easy path, I'd wake up at 60 behind the wheel of a white Land Cruiser wondering whether I could still fit in back home. So I moved to New York to join TechnoServe's Strategic Initiatives team, advising coffee companies on sustainability, and picked up an MBA at Columbia along the way. On a work trip to Cuba, I hatched the idea for [Enveritas](https://enveritas.org) with a longtime colleague.

Enveritas took on a question the certification industry had given up on: how do you verify sustainability conditions on millions of smallholder farms that no auditor will ever visit? We combined satellite imagery, machine learning, and tens of thousands of in-person farm surveys — free of charge to farmers — and went through Y Combinator (S18) along the way. Enveritas now operates in more than 20 countries and grew to a revenue base of more than $20 million.

By then I'd been in coffee for over a decade, and more and more of my free time was going into coding and data science projects. I could feel myself reaching a local maximum. Enveritas was on solid footing, so I traded being the expert in the room for being the beginner again and joined [Protocol Labs](https://protocol.ai) to work on [Hypercerts](https://hypercerts.org), a data standard for rewarding impact in domains as varied as journalism, climate, and open source. That detour took me deep into the world of public goods funding — I became a [Gitcoin steward](https://go.gitcoin.co/blog/introducing-the-community-stewards-program) and published a small mountain of analyses on retroactive funding experiments — and it kept turning up the same question I'd been chasing since Tanzania: did any of this actually work?

That question became [Kariba Labs](https://www.karibalabs.co/). We build [Open Source Observer](https://www.opensource.observer), an open data platform that helps foundations and ecosystem funds see the open source software they depend on, decide what to support, and check whether the money made a difference. It's the hardest measurement problem I've worked on, which is probably why I like it.

Along the way I've also become a board member of [Cup of Excellence](https://cupofexcellence.org/) and a Vice President at my local PTA (the toughest governance job of the lot).

I live in New York with my wife and two young boys. In the winter, I enjoy skiing in the Catskills and the rest of the year I love having friends over for pizza on the Ooni.

These days I write mostly about measuring impact with data, data visualization, and coffee — plus the occasional detour into travel, mini art projects, or a [bracket](../essays/pizza_bracket) to find the best pizza in NYC.
