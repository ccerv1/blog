Hi, I'm Carl.

**TL;DR:** I've spent the better part of two decades learning to measure things that are hard to measure — from soil erosion in a Tanzanian village, to sustainability on smallholder coffee farms at [Enveritas](https://enveritas.org), to the impact of open source software at [Kariba Labs](https://www.karibalabs.co/), where I build [Open Source Observer](https://www.opensource.observer).

## The short version

I started college on a computer science track, switched to environmental science, and wrote a thesis that used satellite data to predict soil erosion in a Tanzanian village. That took me back to Tanzania on a self-styled "DIY Peace Corps," and then to [TechnoServe](https://www.technoserve.org/), where I spent years helping Ethiopian coffee cooperatives build the systems and secure the financing they needed to sell great coffee. I went on to cofound [Enveritas](https://enveritas.org) to verify sustainability on coffee farms at scale, then joined [Protocol Labs](https://protocol.ai) to work on [Hypercerts](https://hypercerts.org), a data standard for rewarding impact. Today I run [Kariba Labs](https://www.karibalabs.co/), where we build [Open Source Observer](https://www.opensource.observer) to measure the impact of open source software in the open.

## The long version

My path has been anything but linear. I spent my first two years of college on a computer science track before switching to environmental science, with a minor in a field called Science & Society — a blend of the philosophy of science and economics. Looking back, that switch is the whole story in miniature: I wanted to point technical tools at messy, human, real-world problems rather than abstract ones. My [thesis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/253841943_Estimating_Soil_Loss_Using_ASTER_and_Diffuse_Reflectance_Spectrometry_a_Case_Study_of_the_Village_of_Kambi_ya_Simba_in_Northern_Tanzania's_Rift_Valley_Highlands) used remote sensing and soil spectrometry to predict erosion in a Tanzanian village — my first taste of a question I'd keep chasing for the next twenty years: how do you measure something important that nobody can easily see?

I raised a few grants to go back to that village after graduation, which became what I fondly call my "DIY Peace Corps" experience. I learned Swahili and started a microfinance program. I could have studied development from a distance; instead I kept wanting to be at the source, close enough to see whether things actually worked. That instinct — go where the problem is, then build something that helps — has shaped every job since.

It led me to [TechnoServe](https://www.technoserve.org/), where I started out as a volunteer helping coffee cooperatives build accounting and inventory management systems. It turned out that better books could be as powerful as better agronomy: measurement and transparency were what let cooperatives earn trust and unlock financing. I played a key role in securing and implementing a [$65 million grant from the Gates Foundation](https://www.technoserve.org/our-work/projects/coffee-initiative/) and setting up a large cross-functional team in Ethiopia. We helped over 100 cooperatives get [new financing](https://pressroom.ifc.org/all/pages/PressDetail.aspx?ID=22203) and produce wonderful specialty coffees. I later became a founding member of TechnoServe's Strategic Initiatives team in New York, leading partnerships and consulting engagements with a portfolio of coffee companies. I completed an MBA at Columbia during this time and hatched the idea for [Enveritas](https://enveritas.org) while on a work trip to Cuba.

Enveritas was the same problem at a larger scale: how do you verify sustainability conditions across millions of smallholder coffee farms that no auditor could ever hope to visit? We built a technology-based platform to do exactly that. It's now active in more than 20 countries and used by leading corporations to monitor their supply chains, and we grew it to a revenue base of more than $20 million — eventually stable enough that I could step away without putting it at risk.

By then I'd been working in coffee for over a decade. Alongside managing a growing team, a quieter shift had been underway: I was spending more and more of my free time coding and building data science projects. I could feel myself reaching a local maximum, and I wanted to lean into that technical curiosity — to find a role that was far more technical and would force me to learn an entirely new domain. I've always liked that particular trade: giving up being the expert in the room for being the least knowledgeable person at the table, with everything still to learn. It felt like time for a new one.

That's what took me to [Protocol Labs](https://protocol.ai), where I went deep on the software supply chain and worked on [Hypercerts](https://hypercerts.org), a flexible data standard for rewarding contributions in domains as varied as journalism, climate solutions, and event sponsorship. If Enveritas was about measuring impact in coffee, Hypercerts was that same instinct generalized — an attempt to make "impact" itself into something you could represent, reward, and fund.

Which brings me to [Kariba Labs](https://www.karibalabs.co/). We build technology to measure impact in the open, and our first product is an analytics platform called [Open Source Observer](https://www.opensource.observer) that helps foundations and funds understand and allocate resources to open source software. It's the same question that started with a soil sample in Tanzania, just pointed at a new frontier.

I'm a [Gitcoin steward](https://go.gitcoin.co/blog/introducing-the-community-stewards-program), Y Combinator alum (S18), board member of [Cup of Excellence](https://cupofexcellence.org/), and a Vice President at my local PTA.

I live in New York with my wife and two young boys. In the winter, I enjoy skiing in the Catskills and the rest of the year I love having friends over for pizza on the Ooni.

These days I write mostly about measuring impact with data, data visualization, and coffee — plus the occasional detour into travel, mini art projects, or a bracket to find the best pizza in NYC.
