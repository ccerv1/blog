# The bridge essay (working draft/outline)

> The piece that connects the two careers. Nobody — including me — has written it.
> Once published: Featured #1 on the homepage, linked from every speaker bio,
> the canonical answer to "so what's your deal?"

## Working titles

- Selling blind (probably the winner — see the 2011 quote below)
- From coffee farms to codebases
- Twenty years of measuring things people said couldn't be measured

## Thesis

I've spent twenty years building measurement infrastructure for long-tail
producers whose value everyone claims to care about but nobody actually
measures: smallholder farmers, then open source maintainers. Both are priced
out of the legacy systems (certification, grant applications) that were
supposed to serve them. Both times, the answer was the same: data
infrastructure that's free at the point of use, funded by the demand side.

**The inversion (Carl's own framing, July 2026 — probably the essay's core):**
Coffee and software are mirror-image measurement problems. Coffee has
ruthlessly efficient markets — everything has a price; the hard part is
tracing flows from farmers to end buyers (the problem Enveritas solved).
Software is the opposite: the dependency graph traces the flows for free;
the hard part is valuing the impact, importance, and resilience of the teams
behind them and calibrating funding to keep them building — because there's
no market, and imposing one can distort the very thing you want to sustain.
Same career, two halves: first solve traceability where prices exist, then
solve valuation where they don't.

## Outline

### 1. Open with the 2011 Copenhagen talk

At the Nordic Roaster Forum in 2011, I said: "The farmer is selling blind —
and the exporters are selling blind. A floral Sidamo might pass as a
Yirgacheffe and a low-altitude Yirgacheffe might pass as a Sidamo." 80–90% of
Ethiopian exports were untraceable through the ECX at the time.
(Source: http://nordicbaristacup.com/2011/10/nordic-roaster-forum-2011-talk-carl-cervone/)

I thought I was describing a coffee problem. It took me another decade to
realize I was describing the default condition of every market for
under-measured work — including the software that runs the world.

### 2. The village (2002–2005)

- Thesis in Kambi ya Simba: satellites + soil spectrometry to predict erosion.
- The humbling lesson from the DIY Peace Corps grant proposal: "the report
  only succeeded in quantifying certain problems that farmers are already
  intimately familiar with." Measurement without a market or an institution
  attached is just a report nobody reads.
- Seeds of the pattern: mobile deposit vouchers concept note (2005), two years
  before M-Pesa. Right diagnosis, no distribution.

### 3. Ethiopia (2009–2013): measurement attached to money

- Led TechnoServe's entry into Ethiopia under the $65M Gates-funded Coffee
  Initiative. 64 washing stations, team of dozens (hundreds at harvest).
- The unlock wasn't agronomy — it was making cooperatives *legible to banks*:
  business plans, accounting systems, the $10M IFC risk-sharing facility.
  62 cooperatives / 47,000 farmers financed in a system demanding 100% collateral.
- Receipts: premiums grew from 25% to 45% *after* we left (Triple Line
  evaluation); HKS made it their "system leadership" case study.
- Story beat: Busa Bechane (essay already on the site) — what "results-
  oriented" actually looks like on the ground.

### 4. Enveritas (2016–2022): measuring an entire commodity

- The certification industry's cost model excluded exactly the farmers it
  claimed to serve (<2 ha, <$2/day). We inverted it: free for farmers, paid
  by roasters. Satellites + ML + tens of thousands of farm visits a year.
- The "12.5 million" study: the industry said "25 million coffee farmers" for
  two decades with no methodology. We measured — 20,000+ interviews, 20
  countries — and cut the number in half. Presented to the ICO.
  (https://www.ico.org/documents/cy2018-19/Presentations/statistics-item-3-enveritas.pdf)
- 320,000+ farms visited; Peet's, JDE Peet's, Dunkin'; still growing after I left.

### 5. The turn: software supply chains (2022–present)

- Hypercerts at Protocol Labs → OSO at Kariba Labs.
- The rhyme, stated plainly: maintainers are smallholders. Long-tail
  producers, invisible in the data, priced out of the funding systems built
  in their name. "The farmer is selling blind" → the maintainer is too.
- Same playbook: open, free-at-point-of-use measurement infrastructure,
  funded by the demand side (foundations, ecosystem funds).
- RetroPGF/Optimism work as *evidence of the thesis*, not the identity.

### 6. Close

- What I believe now about measurement: it only matters when it's attached to
  money and institutions; folklore numbers survive until someone does the
  boring work; the long tail is where the impact is.
- Characteristic closing, short and forward: the coffee industry eventually
  did the census. Software hasn't yet. That's the hill I'm climbing.
